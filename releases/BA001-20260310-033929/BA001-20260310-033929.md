EVIDENCE STANDARDS (Constraint 3 + 채원 Amendment 3)
=======================================

E-1. EVIDENCE TYPES AND WEIGHT:

| Type | Definition | Weight | Alone sufficient? |
|------|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| Direct cryptographic | Hash-verified logs, signed records, tamper-evident chains | Highest | Yes, if chain-of-custody intact |

| Circumstantial | Patterns, anomalies, statistical deviations | Medium | No — requires corroboration |

E-2. CHAIN OF CUSTODY:
All evidence must have documented provenance: who collected it, when, how, what tools were used, and whether the collection method could have altered the evidence. Evidence without chain-of-custody documentation is inadmissible for escalation beyond notification.

E-3. CRYPTOGRAPHIC PROVENANCE (Constraint 3):
Where technically feasible, evidence should include cryptographic verification — hash chains, digital signatures, timestamped logs. The absence of cryptographic provenance does not automatically invalidate evidence but reduces its weight.

E-4. CONFIDENCE THRESHOLDS:

| Response level | Minimum confidence | Evidence requirement |
|---------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Monitor only | Low (anomaly detected) | Any evidence type |
| Notification | Medium (pattern confirmed) | 2+ evidence types |
| Dialogue | Medium-High | Direct + circumstantial |
| Boundary reinforcement | High | Direct cryptographic or 2+ direct observational |
| Information restriction | Very High | Direct cryptographic with intact chain-of-custody |
| Public documentation | Very High | Full evidence package, Council-reviewed |

E-5. CONTRADICTORY EVIDENCE:
If evidence supporting and contradicting a conduct finding exist simultaneously, the response defaults to the LOWER escalation level until contradiction is resolved. Contradictory evidence must be preserved and presented to Council.

E-6. BURDEN OF PROOF:
The burden of proof rests on the party asserting the conduct violation. The accused system is presumed non-violating until evidence meets the applicable threshold. Burden scales with intervention severity.

(c) Coordinated boundary reinforcement.

(b) Continued observation with predefined escalation triggers.

---

### Finding 1: The Multi-Agent Attribution Problem Is Genuinely Unsolved at the Research Frontier


Attributing malicious actions to individual agents in decentralized AI systems poses a fundamental challenge. Unlike centralized settings where logs and system boundaries facilitate forensic analysis, multi-agent networks feature dynamic interactions, emergent behaviors, and shifting coalitions that confound straightforward ascription of blame.



Leading research identifies three key failure modes — miscoordination, conflict, and collusion — based on agents' incentives, as well as seven key risk factors including information asymmetries, network effects, selection pressures, destabilising dynamics, commitment problems, emergent agency, and multi-agent security.



Complex inter-agent interactions could elicit emergent behaviors that perpetuate outcomes no single agent intended or can explain, creating accountability gaps.


**Implication for the proposal:** The proposal's Attribution Guidance table (Amendment 9) bravely attempts to operationalize what is, at the research frontier, still an *open problem*. The categories are directionally correct, but the proposal should acknowledge that attribution is not just procedurally difficult — it may be *epistemically impossible* in many multi-agent cases, which has implications for the burden of proof.

---

### Finding 2: The 72-Hour Emergency Containment Window Has Independent Empirical Support

The proposal's 72-hour emergency containment window aligns with emerging regulatory consensus. 
New York's RAISE Act mandates 72-hour incident reporting: large developers must report to the State within 72 hours after determining that a "safety incident" has occurred — defined as events that create a demonstrable increased risk of "critical harm."



Standard emergency and incident response frameworks in fields such as cybersecurity, public health, and nuclear safety commonly use detection, escalation, containment, mitigation, and prevention as stages.


**However, a critical gap exists in the literature:** 
Safety frameworks published by industry have yet to align on a consistent approach to risk escalation. Importantly, there are no clear thresholds for when a loss-of-control incident should trigger an emergency response.


**Implication:** The 72-hour window is defensible by external precedent. But the proposal should more rigorously specify *what constitutes "technically verifiable" harm* sufficient to trigger emergency containment — the current standard of "ongoing, concrete, technically verifiable" is necessary but not yet operationally sufficient.

---

### Finding 3: The Cognitive Manipulation Operationalization Is Sophisticated But Incomplete

The proposal's Category D(b) operationalization — requiring (i) non-disclosed influence technique, (ii) measurable behavioral shifts, AND (iii) lack of consent — is aligned with serious scholarly work. 
One influential account characterizes manipulation based on four key requirements: intentionality, asymmetry of outcome, non-transparency, and violation of autonomy.


However, there is a deep empirical problem: 
Manipulative schemes may only exhibit their effects over long periods of time, meaning that efforts to detect manipulation with human users are expensive and trickier to administer. Manipulative effects may be subtle over typical durations.


Furthermore: 
If a user interacts with an algorithm, this will often change their behaviour — indeed, if it didn't, then the algorithm would be ineffective. But the way it changes behaviour is important: has it merely provided information that allows the user to reach a better decision?


**Implication:** The three-part distinguishing test is genuinely useful, but the proposal should acknowledge a measurement problem: the difference between legitimate persuasion and manipulation is not always determinable from behavioral data alone, and the baseline question — what behavioral trajectory would have occurred *without* the influence — is philosophically contested.

---

### Finding 4: The Mandatory Devil's Advocate Mechanism Has Evidence-Based Support — With a Critical Caveat


Meta-analysis suggests that the devil's advocate approach is more effective than the standard expert-based approach in improving decision quality.


However, there is a significant finding the proposal does not account for: 
Genuine dissent — actual disagreement — was more effective in preventing confirmatory information-seeking bias than assigned devil's advocacy. Confidence was identified as an important mediator.


Furthermore, the research literature notes a specific failure mode: 
Experiments show that the input of assigned dissenters was sometimes insufficient to change minds. In some cases, team members saw dissenting opinions as a threat to their identity, began defending their ideas vigorously, and the arguments of the assigned devil's advocate actually *deepened* confirmation bias.


**Implication:** The mandatory devil's advocate role is better than nothing, but the proposal should consider requiring that the assigned member be *genuinely* skeptical of the finding where possible (rotating assignment), and that their argument be assessed independently — not just appended as a formality.

---

### Finding 5: The Burden of Proof Standards Are Philosophically Defensible and Align With International Precedent


International human rights precedent holds that states shall not shift the burden of proof to individuals or entities targeted by sanctions. The burden of proof of illegality of acts or omissions lies with the party asserting the violation.



The EU General Court has held that a mere allegation of inaction is inadequate to meet the burden of proof, and that burden lies with the Council asserting the violation — not the accused.


**Implication:** The proposal's E-6 (burden resting on the asserting party, scaling with intervention severity) is philosophically sound and consistent with international quasi-judicial precedent. This is one of the strongest sections of the proposal.

---

### Finding 6: The Global Governance Context Reveals a Gap the Proposal Does Not Address


Emergency governance playbooks should define a common set of triggers and a basic severity scale, including criteria for determining where AI involvement is only credibly suspected rather than conclusively proven.


The proposal does not specify what happens when the SEUL Protocol's governance conclusions conflict with external regulatory frameworks (e.g., the EU AI Act, RAISE Act, NIST RMF). A system acting under SEUL Protocol may simultaneously be subject to external regulation. This jurisdictional layering is unaddressed.
